Common Questions About Whole Home Remodels

The answers you need before making this decision

How long does a whole home remodel take?
Most whole home remodels take 6-12 months of construction, depending on scope and complexity. A first floor remodel might be 4-6 months. A complete gut renovation of a large historic home could extend beyond a year. Design and permitting typically add 3-4 months before construction begins. We create detailed schedules during planning so you know what to expect.
Do we need to move out during construction?
For comprehensive whole home projects, we typically recommend finding temporary housing. This allows work to proceed more efficiently, keeps your family comfortable, and often shortens the overall timeline. For more limited renovations - like a first floor remodel - living on site may be feasible with some adjustments. We'll discuss logistics during your discovery call.
Is it cheaper to renovate or buy a different house?
It depends on your situation. If you love your location, have good bones in your current home, and want something truly custom, renovation often makes financial and emotional sense. Moving costs, real estate commissions, and the premium for move-in ready homes add up quickly. Plus, you rarely get exactly what you want when buying - there's always compromise. We can help you evaluate whether renovation is the right investment for your circumstances.
Can you renovate a historic home without losing its character?
Absolutely - this is one of our specialties. The North Shore is full of antique Colonials, Victorians, and Federal-era homes that deserve thoughtful updates. The key is understanding what makes the home special and designing updates that complement rather than compete with original features. We work within historic district guidelines while creating homes that function beautifully for modern life. Learn more about our historic home expertise →
How do you handle all the different trades and schedules?
This is where design-build shines. We coordinate all subcontractors, material deliveries, inspections, and schedules. You have one point of contact and one team accountable for the entire project. No finger-pointing between architect, general contractor, and subs. No wondering who's responsible when something goes wrong. Just clear communication and results.
What if you find problems when you open the walls?
Older homes sometimes have surprises: outdated wiring, inadequate framing, previous repairs done poorly, or hidden water damage. When we encounter issues, we document them, discuss options and costs with you, and don't proceed without your approval. Our thorough discovery process and experience with older homes helps minimize surprises, but we always plan for contingencies.
