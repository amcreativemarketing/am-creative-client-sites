What a Kitchen Remodel with Clarke Building Actually Includes

Every kitchen project is different. Some homeowners need a complete tear-out and rebuild. Others are happy with the bones and just need updated surfaces. Clarke Building handles the full range - here's what each type of work involves.

Full Gut Renovation

A full gut renovation means stripping the kitchen back to the studs and starting over. Everything comes out - cabinets, countertops, flooring, drywall, electrical, plumbing - and goes back in to a new plan built around your layout preferences and how the space connects to the rest of your home. This is the right choice when the current kitchen is beyond cosmetic improvement, when you're changing the footprint, or when systems like wiring and plumbing need to be brought up to code. Clarke Building coordinates all trades under one roof and manages the sequence so nothing gets out of order.

Cabinet Refacing

If your cabinet boxes are structurally sound and the layout works, refacing is a practical way to modernize the kitchen without the cost or disruption of a full replacement. Clarke Building replaces the door fronts, drawer fronts, and hardware, and applies new veneer or solid wood to the exposed cabinet frames. The result looks like new cabinetry at a fraction of the full replacement cost. This works well for homeowners who are happy with their storage layout but want the kitchen to look and feel current.

Countertop Replacement

Countertops take more abuse than any other surface in the kitchen, and replacing them is one of the highest-impact upgrades you can make. Clarke Building installs quartz, granite, quartzite, butcher block, and solid surface countertops, and works with your preferred fabricator or can recommend one. The process includes removal of the existing top, any necessary substrate repairs, templating, and final installation with tight seams and proper overhang. Countertop replacement is often paired with a backsplash update and new sink or faucet - Clarke can coordinate all of it.

Layout Changes and Structural Work

A kitchen that doesn't flow right isn't fixed by new cabinets - it's fixed by changing the layout. Clarke Building handles wall removals, beam installations, island additions and relocations, and other structural changes that open up a kitchen and make it work better. This type of work requires permits, engineering in some cases, and careful coordination with mechanical trades. Dave scopes this work accurately from the start so there are no discoveries mid-project that change the budget.

Appliance Upgrades and Coordination

New appliances often drive a kitchen remodel - a range that requires a new gas line, a hood that needs custom framing, a refrigerator that changes the cabinet configuration. Clarke Building coordinates appliance selection with the rest of the design so rough-in locations, electrical circuits, and cabinet sizing are all planned together before demo starts. Clarke handles the rough-in coordination with licensed plumbers and electricians and manages the final appliance installation. You don't have to figure out which contractor does what - that's already handled.

What Does a Kitchen Remodel Cost on the North Shore?

Pricing is the first thing most homeowners want to know, and it's a fair question. Kitchen remodeling on the North Shore typically ranges from $25,000 to $80,000 depending on the scope of work, the materials selected, and whether any structural or mechanical changes are involved.

Tier 1

Cabinet Refacing and Countertop Refresh

$25K - $35K

Surface-level upgrades: new cabinet doors and hardware, a countertop replacement, a new backsplash, and possibly a new sink or faucet. The layout stays the same, no walls move, and the major appliances are not replaced. A meaningful improvement without the disruption of a full renovation.

Tier 2

New Cabinets, Countertops, and Layout Adjustments

$35K - $60K

Full cabinet replacement, new countertops and backsplash, updated lighting, and minor layout adjustments - moving a non-load-bearing wall, adding or resizing an island, or reconfiguring the pantry. Mechanical trades are typically involved for updated electrical and plumbing rough-ins.

Tier 3

Full Gut Renovation with Layout Changes

$60K - $80K+

Complete demo, structural changes, new layout, custom or semi-custom cabinetry, premium countertop materials, a full appliance package, new flooring, and updated mechanical systems. These are significant projects that transform how the kitchen functions and connects to the rest of the home.

Pricing Philosophy

These ranges reflect real North Shore project costs - not a number designed to get you to sign, with the real price revealed later. Clarke Building does not submit lowball bids. The estimate Dave puts together after walking your kitchen is based on the actual scope of work. If something changes during the project, you'll hear about it before it happens, not when you get the bill.

The only way to get an accurate number for your kitchen is to have Dave walk the space. Every kitchen is different, and a pricing formula won't account for what's behind your walls, the materials you want, or what you're trying to accomplish.

How a Kitchen Remodel Works with Clarke Building

Kitchen remodeling has a lot of moving parts - trades, materials, lead times, permits. Here's exactly how Clarke Building runs a project from the first call to the last punch list item.

"Dave went over all our options thoroughly. We knew exactly what we were getting before anything started."
- Kathy M., West Newbury, MA
1

Initial Walkthrough and Conversation

Before any numbers get discussed, Dave walks your kitchen in person. He's looking at the existing layout, the condition of the cabinets and surfaces, what the mechanical situation looks like, and how the kitchen connects to adjacent spaces. More importantly, he's listening - to what's not working, what you actually need, and what success looks like for this project.

2

Scope Definition and Estimate

After the walkthrough, Dave puts together a written scope and estimate that reflects what you discussed. This is not a preliminary ballpark - it's a detailed account of the work to be done, organized by phase. If there are options to consider (cabinet refacing vs. replacement, for example), those are presented with separate pricing so you can make an informed decision. You'll know what you're agreeing to before anything is signed.

3

Design Coordination and Material Selection

Once scope and budget are agreed on, the focus shifts to materials and design decisions. Cabinetry, countertop material and edge profile, backsplash, hardware, fixtures, flooring - all of these get locked in before demo starts. Clarke Building can coordinate with your designer or work directly with you on selections. Lead times on custom cabinetry and specialty materials are factored into the schedule, so nothing stalls the project mid-stream.

4

Demo and Rough-In Trades

Demo day is exactly what it sounds like: the existing kitchen comes out. Clarke Building manages the full demo and disposal, then brings in licensed plumbers and electricians for rough-in work - new drain and supply locations, updated electrical circuits, gas rough-in if applicable. This phase also includes any structural work: wall openings, beam installation, subfloor repairs. Inspections are scheduled and completed before the project moves to the next phase.

5

Cabinet and Countertop Installation

With rough-ins inspected and walls closed, cabinetry goes in first - set level, shimmed, and secured to the wall framing. Countertop templating happens after cabinets are installed and confirmed in final position. The fabricated tops come back for installation, followed by backsplash tile, undermount sink setting, and any trim or filler work that completes the cabinet layout. Clarke Building handles the installation directly - this is not handed off to a subcontractor.

6

Appliance Install and Finish Work

With cabinets and countertops in place, appliances are installed and connected. Finish electrical (outlet covers, under-cabinet lighting, fixture installation) and finish plumbing (faucet, disposal, dishwasher) are completed by the licensed trades. Paint or finish work on walls, trim, and ceiling happens during this phase as well. This is when the kitchen starts to look finished, and Clarke Building stays on-site to coordinate the sequencing.

7

Punch List and Final Walkthrough

Before the project closes, Dave walks the kitchen with you and a punch list in hand. Every item that needs to be addressed - a door that doesn't close quite right, a gap that needs caulk, a drawer that needs adjustment - gets noted and corrected before the final invoice is issued. The final walkthrough is not a formality. It's the standard that determines when the project is actually done.
