ADU Law in Massachusetts

In August 2024, Governor Healey signed the Affordable Homes Act into law. Section 8 of this legislation changed the Massachusetts Zoning Act to allow accessory dwelling units up to 900 square feet (or half the gross floor area of the primary home, whichever is smaller) to be built by right in single-family zoning districts statewide. The ADU provisions took full effect on February 2, 2025.

What "by right" means in practice: municipalities can no longer require special permits or discretionary zoning approvals to deny you from building a qualifying ADU on your property. Towns can still apply reasonable requirements like setbacks, building height limits, and site plan review, but they cannot block the construction of a first ADU that meets the size and definition requirements.

This is a significant change. Before this law, many Massachusetts towns either prohibited ADUs entirely or made them extremely difficult to get approved. If you've been thinking about adding a unit for rental income, aging parents, or an adult child, the path to getting it built is now much clearer. Permits & Compliance

In most cases, yes. In Massachusetts, any project that involves structural changes, electrical work, plumbing modifications, or changes to the building envelope requires a permit. That includes kitchen remodels with new plumbing or electrical, home additions, ADUs, deck construction, and new builds. Even interior renovations that move walls or change load paths require permits.

Unpermitted work creates serious problems down the road. If you sell your home, unpermitted work can stall a closing, lower your sale price, or require costly corrections. Clarke Building pulls all necessary permits and schedules every required inspection. Homeowners should never skip this step.

Clarke Building handles all permit applications as the licensed contractor. In Massachusetts, the licensed contractor listed on the permit is responsible for ensuring the work meets code. You, as the homeowner, are typically listed as the property owner on the permit application. Dave prepares the paperwork, submits it to your local building department, and coordinates any plan reviews or zoning questions that come up. Once the permit is issued, Clarke manages the inspection schedule throughout the project so nothing gets missed.

Timelines vary based on scope, permitting, and material lead times. As a general guideline, here's what homeowners can expect:

  • Kitchen remodel: 6 to 12 weeks
  • Home addition: 3 to 6 months
  • ADU construction: 4 to 8 months
  • New construction: 8 to 14+ months
  • Deck construction: 2 to 6 weeks

These are ranges, not guarantees. Custom material lead times, permit processing, and weather can all affect the schedule. Dave gives you a realistic timeline during the planning phase and communicates updates if anything changes.

Construction is disruptive, and Clarke Building is upfront about that. Depending on the scope, you should expect dust, noise, and certain areas of your home being temporarily unavailable. For kitchen remodels, that means no access to the kitchen for a period of time. For additions or ADUs, exterior work brings equipment, materials staging, and trade activity around the property.

Here's what Clarke Building does to manage it: unaffected areas are protected with plastic barriers and floor coverings. The site is cleaned daily. You'll know in advance which spaces will be impacted and for how long. Dave maintains constant communication so there are no surprises about what's happening on any given day. If you need to plan meals, set up a temporary kitchen, or arrange alternative access, those conversations happen before demo starts.

Working With a MA Contractor

Before you hire any contractor in Massachusetts, ask these questions and verify the answers:

  • Do you hold a current Massachusetts HIC (Home Improvement Contractor) license? Ask for the number and verify it.
  • Do you carry General Liability insurance and Workers' Compensation coverage? Ask for certificates of insurance.
  • Can you provide references from recent local projects?
  • Will you provide a written contract with a detailed scope of work?
  • What is the payment schedule, and how is it tied to project milestones?
  • Who pulls the permits, and who manages the inspection schedule?
  • Do you offer a warranty on workmanship?

A trustworthy contractor will answer all of these without hesitation. See why homeowners choose Clarke Building.

Some warning signs that a contractor may not be the right fit:

  • Requesting a large upfront cash deposit before work begins
  • No written contract or a contract that lacks detail on scope, timeline, and payment
  • Cannot provide a valid HIC license number or proof of insurance
  • Pressure to sign immediately or claims the price is only good "today"
  • An estimate that comes in far below everyone else's, which usually means corners will be cut
  • Reluctance to pull permits or suggestions to skip the permit process
  • No references or unwillingness to share past project examples
